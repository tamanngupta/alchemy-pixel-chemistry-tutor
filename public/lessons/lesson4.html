Why not NaH? Why aldehydes first?

Click highlighted terms to explore them.

The B-H bond is the donor NaBH4 does not throw loose H- into solution. The electron pair in a B-H sigma bond is donated into the carbonyl pi star orbital, the antibonding orbital of C=O. As that overlap develops, hydrogen becomes part of the new C-H bond and the C=O pi electrons move onto oxygen.
Protonation is cleanup The first step makes an alkoxide. Water or alcohol solvent then gives oxygen a proton. The proton does not start the attack. It only neutralizes oxygen after carbon has already gained the new C-H bond.
Why NaBH4 is selective NaBH4 is mild enough to reduce aldehydes and ketones but not most esters or amides. If a molecule contains both an aldehyde and an ester, NaBH4 usually reduces the aldehyde and leaves the ester alone. Ketones react more slowly than aldehydes because the carbonyl carbon is more crowded and less electrophilic.
Why NaH is not the reagent Sodium hydride is a powerful base, not a useful carbonyl reducer. The filled 1s orbital on hydride is compact. It overlaps well with the hydrogen end of an H-X sigma star orbital, so it removes protons efficiently. It overlaps poorly with the larger carbon contribution to the carbonyl pi star orbital, so it does not reduce aldehydes or ketones the way BH4- does.
// INTUITION LOCK

Reduction is still nucleophilic addition, but the electron pair comes through a B-H bond. Carbon gains H, oxygen receives the pi electrons, and only after that does the solvent protonate oxygen to give the alcohol.

// STEP BY STEP MECHANISM

Select a step to see its diagram

1
A B-H bond delivers hydride character to carbon▾
The filled B-H sigma bond overlaps with the empty carbonyl pi star orbital. Electron density flows into the carbon side of that pi star orbital, so a new C-H bond forms. At the same time, the C=O pi electrons move onto oxygen, giving an alkoxide. Boron is left electron-poor, which helps pull the transfer forward.
Hydride reduction is an orbital match: filled B-H sigma donates into empty C=O pi star. The new bond forms at carbon because the carbonyl carbon is the electrophilic end.
2
The alkoxide is protonated▾
The oxygen anion picks up H+ from ethanol, water, or acidic work-up. That gives the alcohol. The protonation happens after the C-H bond has already formed.
The proton is not the attacker. It is the finishing touch that turns O- into OH.
3
One borohydride can reduce more than one carbonyl▾
BH4- contains four B-H bonds, so more than one hydride transfer is possible. In real reactions it is not perfectly four-for-four, but NaBH4 still reduces aldehydes and ketones in good yield because the solvent keeps protonating the alkoxide products.
BH4- is like a small hydride reservoir. Each transfer spends one B-H bond and makes one new C-H bond.
4
NaBH4 versus NaH versus LiAlH4▾
NaH behaves as a base because compact hydride overlaps well with the sigma star orbital of H-X bonds. NaBH4 works as a mild hydride donor because its B-H sigma bond can donate into the carbonyl pi star orbital. LiAlH4 is stronger and must be kept dry; it can reduce esters, acids, amides, and nitriles too.
Not every compound with hydrogen attached to a metal is a carbonyl reducer. The orbital fit decides whether hydrogen removes a proton or adds to carbonyl carbon.

Why mild acid is best Acid activates the carbonyl and helps turn OH into water, a good leaving group. Too much acid protonates the amine, removing the nitrogen lone pair. Too little acid leaves the carbonyl under-activated.
Addition then elimination This starts like nucleophilic addition but ends differently. After nitrogen adds, the OH is converted into water and leaves. An R2C=N-Z bond forms, so the oxygen is replaced by a nitrogen group.
// INTUITION LOCK

Nitrogen additions are condensations: add the nucleophile, then throw away water. The carbonyl oxygen does not stay. That is why oximes, hydrazones, and imines are not alcohols.

// STEP BY STEP MECHANISM

Select a step to see its diagram

1
Nitrogen lone pair attacks carbonyl carbon▾
The nitrogen atom donates its lone pair into the carbonyl carbon. The C=O pi electrons move to oxygen, giving a tetrahedral addition product called a carbinolamine.
Same first move as before: electron-rich atom attacks electron-poor carbon. The nucleophile is nitrogen, not a proton.
2
Proton transfers set up water as leaving group▾
The OH group is protonated to become OH2+. Water is a much better leaving group than OH-. At the same time, nitrogen must keep access to its electron pair so the C=N bond can form.
Acid is not here to attack. Acid edits the leaving group so the molecule can lose water cleanly.
3
Water leaves and C=N forms▾
Water leaves. The nitrogen lone pair helps form the R2C=N-Z bond, giving an imine, oxime, hydrazone, semicarbazone, or 2,4-DNP derivative depending on H2N-Z.
Addition gave the molecule a handle. Elimination removes water and replaces C=O with R2C=N-Z.
4
Why pH 4-5 is the sweet spot▾
If the solution is too acidic, H2N-Z becomes H3N+-Z and loses nucleophilicity. If it is too basic, the carbonyl is not activated and OH is not converted into water. Mild acid balances both jobs.
Too much acid silences nitrogen. Too little acid leaves the door closed. Mild acid keeps both partners useful.
