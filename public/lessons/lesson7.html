The Enolate Files

// INTRODUCTION

Aldol Condensation

// THE REACTION

What is Aldol Condensation?

The name is a portmanteau: aldehyde plus alcol. When an aldehyde carrying at least one alpha-hydrogen reacts with dilute base under mild warming, two molecules couple at the alpha-carbon of one and the carbonyl carbon of the other. A carbon-carbon bond forms — one of the most fundamental bond constructions in organic chemistry.

The alpha-hydrogen is essential. Without a hydrogen on the carbon adjacent to the carbonyl, the reaction cannot proceed. Ketones can react too, but the equilibrium is far less favourable — their carbonyl carbon is more hindered and less electrophilic due to two electron-donating alkyl groups.

// SCOPE

1Two identical or different aldehydes with alpha-H (self-condensation or mixed)
2Two identical or different ketones (less favourable, equilibrium lies left)
3One aldehyde and one ketone
4Crossed aldol: one partner has no alpha-H and acts only as the electrophile
// BASE OPTIONS
Dilute NaOH | Aqueous K₂CO₃ | Alkali metal alkoxides | Alkali carbonate

// MECHANISM — STEP 1

OH⁻ is added. What does it do?

Hydroxide is added to acetaldehyde. It carries a lone pair and a full negative charge. In this system it can play two roles: nucleophile (attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon directly) or base (abstracts a proton from the molecule).

Which role does OH⁻ take in this reaction?

OH⁻ acts as a base here. Proton transfer is kinetically preferred — the activation energy for abstracting the alpha-H is lower than for nucleophilic addition to the carbonyl. The alpha-H is unusually acidic (pKa ~17-20) because loss of the alpha-H generates a carbanion that is immediately stabilised by the adjacent carbonyl through resonance — the electrons delocalise into the pi system. The base abstracts that proton because it can, and because the resulting anion is stable enough to persist.
The alpha-H is the weak link in the chain. The carbonyl next door turns an ordinary C-H into an acidic one by giving the resulting anion somewhere to put its electrons.

// MECHANISM — STEP 2

The carbanion stabilises. How?

After OH⁻ abstracts the alpha-H from CH₃CHO, a lone pair appears on the alpha carbon. This carbanion would be highly unstable in isolation. But it is not isolated — it sits directly next to a C=O group.

The lone pair on the alpha carbon is in a p-orbital that is perfectly aligned with the pi system of the adjacent carbonyl. The electrons delocalise into the pi* antibonding orbital of C=O. The negative charge spreads across two atoms simultaneously.

The result is the enolate anion. It has two resonance forms: one with the charge on the alpha carbon (the carbanion form, which drives reactivity) and one with the charge on the oxygen (more stable, since oxygen is more electronegative and better accommodates negative charge). The true structure is a hybrid of both.

In the enolate, where does the negative charge sit in the more stable resonance form?

The negative charge in the enolate rests predominantly on oxygen in the more stable form. Oxygen has a higher electronegativity than carbon — its nucleus pulls electron density more strongly, making it a better home for a lone pair. The enolate is written with O⁻ in the more stable resonance structure. But — the carbon form is where nucleophilic reactivity comes from. The enolate attacks through carbon in the next step.
Stability and reactivity live at different ends of the enolate. Oxygen stabilises the charge. Carbon does the attacking. The two resonance forms tell you both stories at once.

// MECHANISM — STEP 3

The enolate attacks. Where?

The enolate of acetaldehyde is CH₂=CH-O⁻ (oxygen form) or equivalently ⁻CH₂-CHO (carbon form). The nucleophilic carbon of the enolate will attack a second molecule of acetaldehyde.

Which site of the second acetaldehyde does the enolate carbon attack?

The carbonyl carbon of the second aldehyde carries a delta+ charge because the electronegative oxygen pulls electron density through the C=O bond. It is the electrophilic site. The enolate carbon attacks this carbon. The enolate HOMO attacks the carbonyl pi* LUMO of the second aldehyde molecule. A new C-C bond forms and the carbonyl pi bond breaks, with electrons moving onto the oxygen to give an alkoxide intermediate.
The enolate is ambident — it can react at carbon or oxygen. In aldol, carbon does the attacking because hard electrophiles (like the carbonyl carbon) prefer hard nucleophiles, and the carbon end is the harder nucleophilic site.

// MECHANISM — STEP 4

Protonation gives the aldol product

After C-C bond formation, the intermediate is an alkoxide (O⁻). Water in the reaction mixture donates a proton to this oxygen. The product is the aldol — a beta-hydroxy aldehyde. No atoms have been lost yet. This step completes the addition phase.

What type of reaction has occurred up to this point, before any heating?

No atoms have left the molecule at this stage. The enolate added across the carbonyl pi bond of the second aldehyde. Both starting molecules are fully incorporated into the product. This is a nucleophilic addition. The term condensation is often misapplied to this step. The aldol product can be isolated cleanly using mild conditions such as aqueous K₂CO₃ at low temperature.
Addition and condensation are two different events. Addition: atoms joined, nothing lost. Condensation: atoms joined, water lost. These are separable under the right conditions.

// MECHANISM — STEP 5

Apply heat. The aldol loses water.

The aldol (3-hydroxybutanal) has an OH on the beta carbon and a hydrogen on the alpha carbon. On heating with acid or base, these are eliminated as water. This dehydration is the condensation step — the event that gives the full reaction its name.

What forms when the aldol is heated, and why is that product thermodynamically stable?

The beta-OH and the alpha-H are eliminated as water in an E1cb-type process under basic conditions. The product is crotonaldehyde (but-2-enal, CH₃-CH=CH-CHO). The C=C double bond sits between C2 and C3, directly conjugated with the C=O at C1. This extended conjugation delocalises pi electrons across all four atoms, significantly lowering the energy relative to the unconjugated aldol.
The reaction is now called aldol condensation. Condensation = addition followed by elimination of water.
Conjugation is the thermodynamic engine of the dehydration. Every pi overlap between adjacent orbitals lowers energy. The product wants to be conjugated — that is why water loss is spontaneous on heating.

// BONUS QUESTION

Crossed aldol: benzaldehyde + acetaldehyde

Benzaldehyde (C₆H₅CHO) has no alpha-hydrogen. The carbon attached to its CHO is part of the aromatic ring — those C-H bonds are not alpha-H in the aldol sense. Acetaldehyde (CH₃CHO) has three alpha-H atoms on its methyl group.

Where does the enolate form, and why does it attack benzaldehyde specifically?

Enolate formation requires an alpha-H. Benzaldehyde has none, so it cannot form an enolate. The enolate always comes from acetaldehyde. Benzaldehyde acts purely as the electrophile. Its carbonyl carbon is actually more electrophilic than acetaldehyde's because there is no alkyl group providing electron donation, and the aromatic ring withdraws electron density through the pi system. After dehydration, the product is cinnamaldehyde — a fully conjugated system with the phenyl, the vinyl bridge, and the carbonyl all in one connected pi network.
Crossed aldol gives a clean product only when one partner cannot form an enolate. Aryl aldehydes and formaldehyde are the standard enolate-free electrophiles used in synthesis for exactly this reason.

// SUMMARY

Salient features of aldol condensation

Dehydration on heating: Aldols dehydrate readily to alpha,beta-unsaturated carbonyl compounds on heating with acid or base. The product is stabilised by extended conjugation between C=C and C=O.
Strong alkali causes resin formation: In concentrated NaOH, repeated condensation and dehydration chain together, building long conjugated polymers called resins. The newly formed alpha,beta-unsaturated product undergoes further Michael-type additions.
Electronic effects on the carbonyl: Electron-withdrawing groups on the carbonyl carbon promote the condensation by increasing electrophilicity. Electron-donating groups (such as alkyl) reduce it. This is why ketones are less reactive than aldehydes.
Equilibrium favours aldehydes over ketones: For ketone self-condensation, the equilibrium lies far to the left. Diacetone alcohol from acetone requires boiling with solid Ba(OH)₂ in an apparatus that continuously removes the product to shift the equilibrium.

Enolate Resonance Structures
// ACETALDEHYDE — AFTER ALPHA-H ABSTRACTION

When OH⁻ removes the alpha-H from CH₃CHO, a lone pair appears on the alpha carbon. That lone pair is in a p-orbital directly aligned with the pi system of the adjacent C=O. The electrons immediately delocalise. Below are the two resonance forms of the resulting enolate.

In the carbanion form (⁻CH₂-CHO), the negative charge sits on carbon and the C=O double bond remains intact. In the oxygen form (CH₂=CH-O⁻), the negative charge has moved onto oxygen and a new C=C pi bond has formed between the alpha carbon and the carbonyl carbon. The true enolate is a resonance hybrid. The C-C bond has partial double bond character and the C-O bond has partial single bond character.

// KEY NOTE

Industrial Applications

Aldol condensation builds molecules that matter at scale.

// APPLICATION 01

Beta-ionone (Vitamin A precursor)

Citral + acetone → psi-ionone → beta-ionone

Citral (an alpha,beta-unsaturated aldehyde from lemongrass) undergoes crossed aldol condensation with acetone in sodium ethoxide to give psi-ionone. Treatment with BF₃ in acetic acid triggers ring closure to give beta-ionone, the key intermediate in industrial Vitamin A synthesis. Beta-ionone also gives violets their characteristic scent.

// APPLICATION 02

2-Ethylhexanol

Plasticiser precursor, millions of tonnes annually

n-Butanal undergoes self-aldol condensation to give 2-ethylhex-2-enal, which is hydrogenated over Ni to give 2-ethylhexanol. This is esterified with phthalic acid to make dioctyl phthalate (DOP), the plasticiser in PVC. Global production exceeds 2 million tonnes per year.

// APPLICATION 03

Mesityl oxide and Phorone

Acid-catalysed acetone condensation

Acetone with dry HCl gives mesityl oxide [(CH₃)₂C=CH-CO-CH₃]. A second condensation with another acetone molecule gives phorone [(CH₃)₂C=CH-CO-CH=C(CH₃)₂] — a doubly unsaturated diketone. Distilling acetone over H₂SO₄ gives mesitylene (1,3,5-trimethylbenzene) via trimerisation and aromatisation.

// APPLICATION 04

Monosaccharide formation

Glycolaldehyde self-condensation under base

Glycolaldehyde (HOCH₂CHO) undergoes repeated aldol-type condensation under basic conditions to give monosaccharides including glyceraldehyde, erythrose, and tetroses. This reaction has significant prebiotic chemistry relevance — it demonstrates how simple two-carbon units could have assembled into sugars on early Earth.

// APPLICATION 05

n-Butanol synthesis

2 CH₃CHO → crotonaldehyde → n-C₄H₉OH

Two molecules of acetaldehyde undergo aldol condensation to give crotonaldehyde (CH₃-CH=CH-CHO), which is then catalytically hydrogenated over Ni to give n-butanol. Industrially, acetaldehyde comes from ethylene oxidation (Wacker process). n-Butanol is a solvent and fuel additive.

// APPLICATION 06

Resin formation

Strong alkali, repeated condensation chain

In concentrated NaOH, the alpha,beta-unsaturated aldol product undergoes further Michael-type addition with more enolate. Repeated cycles chain molecules into long conjugated polymers — resins. Acetaldehyde under strong OH⁻ gives a dark sticky resin rapidly. This is why industrial aldol reactions are run with dilute base, controlled temperature, and often continuous product removal.
