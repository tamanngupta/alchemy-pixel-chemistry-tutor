C=OThe carbonyl group
Carbonyl group+
A carbon atom double bonded to an oxygen atom (C=O). The most important functional group in organic chemistry. It shows up in aldehydes, ketones, carboxylic acids, and more.
Sigma bond (σ)+
Formed by direct head-on overlap of orbitals. The first bond between any two atoms. Relatively strong and symmetric. In C=O, this is the foundation of the double bond.
Pi bond (π)+
Formed by sideways overlap of p orbitals above and below the bond axis. The second bond in a double bond. More exposed than a sigma bond. This is where the chemistry happens.
sp² hybridization+
The carbonyl carbon uses three hybrid orbitals for sigma bonds, leaving one pure p orbital for the pi bond. This makes the carbonyl group completely flat and planar. The geometry matters for how it reacts.
Electronegativity+
A measure of how strongly an atom pulls electrons toward itself in a bond. Oxygen (3.44) is significantly more electronegative than carbon (2.55). This difference is everything.

// THE FULL PICTURE

That δ+ on carbon is the whole story. Carbon is electron-poor, and that makes it an electrophile. It has an open invitation for anything that can donate electrons.

An electrophile is an electron-loving species. The name comes from "electro" (electron) and "phile" (loving). Because carbon carries a δ+, it is electron-poor and actively seeks out species that can donate electrons to it. The carbonyl carbon is one of the most important electrophiles in organic chemistry.

The C=O double bond is both shorter and over twice as strong as a typical C-O single bond. That kind of strength should mean stability. So why does it react so readily? The answer is polarization. The polarized C=O bond gives the carbon atom a degree of positive charge. That charge attracts negatively charged nucleophiles and encourages reaction. Strength and reactivity are simply not the same thing.

Polarization refers to the unequal distribution of electron density across a bond. In C=O, oxygen pulls the shared π electrons toward itself due to its higher electronegativity, creating a δ- on oxygen and a δ+ on carbon. This asymmetry is what drives the reactivity of the carbonyl group.

Carbon does not need to be weak to be reactive. It just needs to be unequal.
