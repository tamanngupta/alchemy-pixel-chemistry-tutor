// QUESTION 1 OF 3

Carbon carries a δ+ and its π* orbital is exposed. Where do you think the nucleophile attacks?

HOMO stands for Highest Occupied Molecular Orbital. LUMO stands for Lowest Unoccupied Molecular Orbital. Reactions happen when the HOMO of one molecule donates electrons into the LUMO of another. In nucleophilic addition, the nucleophile is the HOMO donor and the carbonyl's π* orbital is the LUMO acceptor. The π* has a larger coefficient at carbon, meaning the orbital lobe is bigger there. Bigger lobe means better overlap, and better overlap means a stronger, more favorable interaction.

The Attack

// THE ATTACK SEQUENCE

CN⁻ approaches the carbonyl carbon from a specific angle. Not head-on, not from the side. This is the Bürgi-Dunitz trajectory, approximately 107° from the plane of the carbonyl.

The Bürgi-Dunitz trajectory describes the optimal angle of nucleophilic attack on a carbonyl carbon. It was determined experimentally by Hans-Beat Bürgi and Jack Dunitz by analyzing crystal structures of molecules where a nucleophile and a carbonyl are close together. The angle of approximately 107° represents the path of maximum orbital overlap between the nucleophile's HOMO and the carbonyl's π* LUMO.

As the lone pair on CN⁻ donates into the π*, two things happen simultaneously. The new C-CN sigma bond begins to form. The π bond of the carbonyl begins to break as the electrons that were in the π bond are pushed onto oxygen. Carbon changes geometry in real time.

Carbon starts as sp² hybridized, trigonal planar, with bond angles of 120°. As the new bond forms and the π bond breaks, carbon rehybridizes to sp³, tetrahedral, with bond angles of approximately 109°. Oxygen picks up the electrons from the broken π bond and becomes O⁻. The negative charge has moved from the nucleophile to oxygen.

sp² hybridization means carbon uses three hybrid orbitals to form sigma bonds, leaving one pure p orbital for the π bond. The result is a flat, planar geometry with bond angles of 120°.
sp³ hybridization means carbon uses four hybrid orbitals, one for each sigma bond, with no p orbital left over. The result is a tetrahedral geometry with bond angles of approximately 109.5°.
An alkoxide is an oxygen anion bonded to a carbon substituent, R-O⁻. It forms here when the π bond electrons are pushed onto oxygen during nucleophilic attack. Alkoxides are stronger bases than hydroxide and are very readily protonated by water or acid.

Reversibility+
Cyanohydrin formation is an equilibrium. Dissolving a cyanohydrin in water can regenerate the starting aldehyde or ketone because cyanide is a good leaving group. Aqueous base decomposes cyanohydrins completely.
Why acid is needed+
The immediate product of the nucleophilic attack is an alkoxide, R-O⁻. Acid is added to protonate this alkoxide and give the hydroxyl group of the cyanohydrin product.
An alkoxide is an oxygen anion bonded to a carbon substituent. It is a stronger base than hydroxide and is protonated very readily by water or dilute acid.
Why base is used+
The active nucleophile is CN⁻, not HCN. HCN is a weak acid and dissociates poorly on its own. A catalytic amount of base deprotonates HCN and generates enough CN⁻ to make the reaction practical.
Aldehydes vs ketones+
Cyanohydrin formation works better with aldehydes. The Keq for benzaldehyde is 212, while a typical ketone sits around 28. The reason is steric. Ketones carry two alkyl groups. Aldehydes carry one alkyl group and one hydrogen. Less steric clash means a more favorable equilibrium.
Keq is the equilibrium constant. A higher Keq means the equilibrium favors the products. Keq = 212 for benzaldehyde means the cyanohydrin product is strongly favored at equilibrium.

// QUESTION 3 OF 3

Given what you know about the sp² to sp³ geometry change during nucleophilic addition, which is more reactive toward this type of reaction?

Steric hindrance refers to the repulsion that occurs when large groups are forced close together. In nucleophilic addition, the geometry change from sp² to sp³ forces substituents on carbon closer together. The larger those substituents are, the greater the steric repulsion, and the higher the energy of the transition state.
The transition state is the point of highest energy along the reaction coordinate. It is neither the starting material nor the product. It cannot be isolated. A higher energy transition state means a slower reaction and a less favorable equilibrium.

The Attack

// THE FULL PICTURE

Nucleophilic addition is not random. Every part of it is directed by orbital geometry, charge distribution, and steric environment.

The nucleophile arrives at 107° along the Bürgi-Dunitz trajectory, aimed directly at the larger lobe of the π* orbital at carbon. The HOMO of the nucleophile overlaps with the LUMO of the carbonyl. A new sigma bond forms, the π bond breaks, electrons move to oxygen, and carbon rehybridizes from sp² to sp³. The negative charge that started on the nucleophile ends up on oxygen as an alkoxide.

Whether the reaction is favorable depends on what is attached to the carbonyl carbon. Aldehydes react more readily than ketones because the geometry change from sp² to sp³ causes less steric clash when one substituent is a hydrogen. This is not a special rule to memorize. It follows directly from the geometry.
